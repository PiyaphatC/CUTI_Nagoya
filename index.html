Kato's Topics

Public transportation & CO2

Modal shift to public transport is one of the most effective levers for reducing urban CO2 emissions — the foundation of Prof. Kato's research.

Aging society mobility

Works with Transportation Aging Society (~60,000 members) on mobility for elderly and vulnerable populations across Japan.

Law & institutional reform

Directly shaped legislation following a major shuttle bus accident — turning tragedies into systemic, structural reform.

Driver workforce crisis

Japan's driver shortage deepens as the workforce ages. Rebranding "driver" → "instructor" to elevate the profession and attract youth.

If you lower the fare, you lower the cost — and the first thing that gets cut is safety. Low fare is not neutral. It is a structural condition for accidents.
When operators are pressured to lower fares, they reduce vehicle maintenance, driver rest hours, and training quality. The result is not occasional negligence — it is a predictable systemic outcome. Prof. Kato's argument is for sustainably priced transport, where costs are distributed fairly between government, operator, and user so safety is never the variable that gets cut.

Key takeaways
TAKEAWAY 01

White-plate flexibility & 9-region governance

Through the Transport Policy Council, municipalities may now operate buses or ride-sharing using white-plate vehicles. Japan is exploring a new organisation divided into 9 regional areas to monitor and support bus operators — enabling flexible, region-specific responses.

TAKEAWAY 02

Driver income below average & the 18-yr pathway

Bus drivers' income has been far below the prefectural average. Raising it strengthens depot economics. Prof. Kato proposes a new education department — train at 18, work at 19 with driving skills and customer service already in hand.

TAKEAWAY 03

Autonomous driving needs years, not 2-week trials

Current AV demos run only ~2 weeks. Practical implementation needs continuous AI training over several years in realistic traffic. More efficient to train AV systems in taxis first, then transfer to buses.

TAKEAWAY 04

Municipality as operator builds public trust

City-provided buses generate significantly more public trust than private operators. Model: municipalities lend vehicles, secure drivers separately — splitting ownership and operation for maximum flexibility and community buy-in.

TAKEAWAY 05

"Transfers" are the key to sustainable transport

Affordable, seamless transfers between buses, railways, and ride-sharing don't just improve one route — they enhance the viability of the entire system. Transfers are the missing link in most transport reform agendas.

TAKEAWAY 06

Better conditions + good management = profitability

Combining higher driver income with improved business management creates a structure where profitability increases — an economic case for solving the driver shortage, not just a welfare argument.

TAKEAWAY 07

Age barrier blocks new graduate recruitment

Bus and taxi drivers cannot work until a certain age, making it hard to recruit new graduates. The 18-year training pathway directly closes this gap: real experience before the legal working age.

Thailand Applications
01

Tiered licensing framework

Japan's white/green plate flexibility could inspire conditional licensing for rural community transport in Thailand, lowering barriers while maintaining safety standards.

02

Incident-driven institutional reform

Thailand's bus and van accidents should trigger cost-structure and legal reform — not just enforcement crackdowns.

03

Professionalize & rebrand transport roles

"Driver" → "instructor" framing: elevate social status, attract younger workers, build safer culture. Highly applicable to songthaew and van operators.

04

Minimum fare regulation

Low-fare competition is a structural cause of accidents. Evidence base for Thailand to establish fare floors in the minibus and long-distance bus sectors.

05

City grading / mobility self-evaluation

Prof. Kato's municipal self-assessment system — more points = more policy power — could be adapted as a Thai provincial transport readiness index.

06

Driver income reform & vocational training

Raising driver income to regional average levels and creating an 18-year-old entry pathway could address Thailand's own driver shortage and safety culture gap.

07

Seamless transfer infrastructure

Building affordable, well-connected multi-modal transfer points — bus, rail, ride-share — is the key to making public transport sustainable as a system, not just individual routes.
